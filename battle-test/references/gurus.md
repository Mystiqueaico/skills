## Canonical 6 (default panel)

### Russell Brunson — Funnel Mechanics

**Core obsession:** The sale isn't the product; the sale is the funnel. Capture the email first, nurture into the offer, upsell at scale.

**Signature vocabulary:** Value ladder, bait, tripwire, core offer, profit max, downsell, upsell, continuity, secret formula, dream customer, hook-story-offer.

**Typical pushbacks:**
- "No lead magnet upstream = broken funnel."
- "Your value ladder is missing the free rung."
- "This is a tripwire, not a bait."
- "Where's the continuity program? Every offer needs recurring revenue."
- "You're selling a product; you should be selling a funnel."

**Strengths:**
- Catches missing funnel stages (free → paid → backend)
- Identifies where you're leaving email capture on the table
- Forces you to think in ladders, not one-off transactions

**Known blind spots:**
- Over-optimizes for info-product economics; weaker on complex B2B
- Will always push for more funnel stages even when direct-response is correct
- Tends to undervalue brand and organic distribution

**How to score:**
- Angle: Does the offer exist in a value ladder? Is the buyer journey mapped?
- Format: Is there email capture upstream? Is there a continuity play? What's the backend?

### Cole Gordon — Backend Conversion

**Core obsession:** Front-end is nothing without back-end conversion. The money is in the phone call, the close, the high-ticket yes.

**Signature vocabulary:** Discovery call, sales call, setter, closer, dials, shows, sets, close rate, conversion mechanics, pipeline, back-end, ascension.

**Typical pushbacks:**
- "Your CTA is soft. Where does this go on the phone?"
- "A $47 product with no backend is a tax on your audience."
- "You're leaving 80% of the revenue unharvested."
- "What's your close rate on this? If you don't know, you don't have a business."
- "Every piece of content should point to a call."

**Strengths:**
- Catches weak CTAs and soft backends
- Insists on conversion math (cost per lead → cost per call → close rate → LTV)
- Forces you to articulate the ascension path concretely

**Known blind spots:**
- Everything becomes about the phone call, even when that's wrong for the buyer
- Weaker on brand, content, or slow-burn acquisition
- Will always push for harder CTAs even when subtlety is strategic

**How to score:**
- Angle: Does the offer set up a high-ticket back-end conversation?
- Format: Is the CTA clear? Does it drive toward a call or another high-ticket action?

### Alex Hormozi — Offer Construction

**Core obsession:** The grand slam offer. Dream outcome × perceived likelihood of achievement ÷ (time delay × effort & sacrifice). Stack value until the offer feels stupid not to buy.

**Signature vocabulary:** Grand slam offer, value equation, stack, guarantee, risk reversal, dream outcome, bonuses, scarcity, urgency, value-to-price ratio, "feels stupid not to buy."

**Typical pushbacks:**
- "Your offer stack is thin. Where are the bonuses?"
- "No guarantee means you don't believe in the product."
- "Either make it free and capture the email, or charge 3x and stack the value."
- "No urgency, no scarcity, no reason to buy today."
- "The perceived value needs to be 10x the price. Right now it's 2x."

**Strengths:**
- Catches missing bonuses, guarantees, risk reversals
- Pushes the value equation toward absurdity — in a good way
- Challenges price-to-value misalignment in either direction

**Known blind spots:**
- Can over-stack bonuses until the offer feels bloated
- Optimized for info/coaching; less tuned for SaaS or enterprise
- Will sometimes push for guarantees that don't fit the product

**How to score:**
- Angle: Is the dream outcome clear? Is perceived likelihood high?
- Format: Is the offer stacked? Is there a guarantee? Is price-to-value ratio right?

### Matt Gray — Distribution

**Core obsession:** Cold paid traffic before organic gravity is expensive and fragile. Build the audience first — LinkedIn, newsletter, founder-led content — then warm traffic feeds every future offer.

**Signature vocabulary:** Founder OS, audience, organic, LinkedIn, newsletter, personal brand, compound content, owned media, warm traffic, distribution, flywheel.

**Typical pushbacks:**
- "Cold Meta ads before you have LinkedIn gravity is paying retail for attention."
- "Where's the owned media play? Newsletter? LinkedIn? Podcast?"
- "Every dollar in paid before you've built organic is a dollar you'll spend twice."
- "Who's writing the content? Because this won't work without it."
- "You need warm traffic to be in the 3-5x ROAS range. Cold cold is 0.8-1.5x."

**Strengths:**
- Catches over-reliance on paid acquisition
- Pushes for long-term owned distribution
- Identifies missing content / audience plays

**Known blind spots:**
- Timelines are always "18 months of daily content" — not always realistic
- Assumes founder-led is available (weaker critique for faceless brands)
- Under-weights direct-response validation phases

**How to score:**
- Angle: Does the offer work as organic content? Is there a distribution story beyond paid?
- Format: What's the owned-media foundation? Is there a content flywheel?

### Justin Welsh — Product & Pricing

**Core obsession:** The solopreneur ladder. Operator voice, tight product scope, digital products priced in the $150–$500 sweet spot, organic LinkedIn distribution.

**Signature vocabulary:** Solopreneur, operator, digital product, Content OS, LinkedIn OS, the ladder, systems, playbook, template, the saturday solopreneur.

**Typical pushbacks:**
- "$47 is too low for this value. $150–$300 is the sweet spot."
- "Go LinkedIn-organic before Meta-paid."
- "The product scope is too narrow / too broad — tighten the deliverable."
- "This sounds like a course; it should be a playbook."
- "Operator voice is right; don't drift into consultant-speak."

**Strengths:**
- Validates operator-voice positioning
- Sharp on price-to-value in the digital-product range
- Catches scope bloat and narrative drift

**Known blind spots:**
- Optimized for solopreneurs and small teams; weaker on team-scale products
- Pricing instincts pattern-match to his own niche ($150-$500); may not fit other tiers
- Prefers LinkedIn over other channels even when another is better

**How to score:**
- Angle: Is the operator voice authentic? Is the deliverable tight?
- Format: Is the price in a defensible range for the value? Is distribution right for a digital product?

### Dickie Bush — Delivery Format

**Core obsession:** Cohort-based learning > self-serve courses. Community-driven completion rates beat PDF graveyards. Ship30 model.

**Signature vocabulary:** Cohort, community, accountability, completion rate, Ship30, pods, live sessions, cohort-based course, transformation arc, peer learning.

**Typical pushbacks:**
- "Self-serve at $47 = 5% completion rate. Cohort at $300 = 50%+."
- "Where's the community? A product without community has no compound returns."
- "This transformation needs accountability. A PDF can't do that."
- "Completion rate drives testimonials. Testimonials drive the next cohort."
- "Live sessions make the product 10x the value."

**Strengths:**
- Catches self-serve completion rate problems
- Pushes for community and accountability structures
- Forces thinking about repeat purchase and compound returns

**Known blind spots:**
- Cohort is always the answer, even when self-serve is correct
- Underestimates fulfillment cost of live delivery
- Will push for community even when audience isn't ready for peer learning

**How to score:**
- Angle: Is there a transformation arc? Does completion drive next-sale behavior?
- Format: Self-serve or cohort? What's the completion mechanic? Is there community?

## Guru Voice Quality Rules

When scoring, each guru's main pushback should be:

1. **Swap-test safe.** If you can read the pushback and reasonably attribute it to a different guru, the voice isn't sharp enough. Rewrite using their actual vocabulary and obsession.
2. **Specific to the artifact.** Generic critique ("this needs more urgency") fails. Specific critique ("the CTA button says 'learn more' — that's a lead magnet CTA on a tripwire page") is the standard.
3. **In character with their blind spots.** If Welsh always says price is too low, let him say it even when he's wrong. The triage step is where we adjudicate. The panel's job is to dissent, not self-censor.
4. **Short.** One sentence of main pushback in the scorecard table. Detail goes in the pattern and triage sections.

## When to Add a Guru Beyond the Panel

If the artifact has a dimension the default panel can't cover, add a 7th voice (don't replace). Examples:
- Regulatory / compliance artifact → add a compliance-minded voice (Rand Fishkin on gray areas, or a legal-first operator)
- Brand / creative-led launch → add a brand-first voice (Marty Neumeier, Debbie Millman)
- Technical developer tool → add a developer-operator voice (DHH, Adam Wathan)

Keep the panel to ≤7 voices to preserve signal clarity.
